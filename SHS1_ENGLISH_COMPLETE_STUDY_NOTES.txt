SECTION 1: ORAL LANGUAGE - SPEECH SOUNDS
================================================================================

A. PURE VOWEL SOUNDS (12 in total)

LONG VOWELS (pronounced longer, more stretched):
1. /i:/ - see, sea, peel, green, tree
2. /a:/ - art, park, part, start, car
3. /ɔ:/ - port, tall, sort, call, ball
4. /u:/ - pool, cool, stool, moon, food
5. /ɜ:/ - bird, earth, birth, nurse, work

SHORT VOWELS (pronounced shorter, more crisp):
1. /ɪ/ - pit, sit, fill, lid, dig
2. /e/ - set, bet, let, red, bed
3. /æ/ - man, that, can, bad, sat
4. /ɒ/ - pot, lot, dross, dog, box
5. /ʊ/ - put, bush, pull, book, foot
6. /ʌ/ - bus, hut, cup, mud, sun
7. /ə/ - around, about, teacher, sofa (neutral sound)

PRONUNCIATION TIPS:
- Short vowels are crisp and quick
- Long vowels are drawn out (hold them longer)
- Vowel shapes on your tongue show which vowel you're producing
- Listen carefully to native speakers
- Practice by repeating words aloud

B. CONSONANT SOUNDS

PLOSIVES (air is blocked then released suddenly):
- /p/ - pet, paper, top (voiceless - no vibration)
- /b/ - bet, trouble, rub (voiced - vocal cords vibrate)
- /t/ - team, better, hot (voiceless)
- /d/ - dim, order, bad (voiced)
- /k/ - came, talking, back (voiceless)
- /g/ - game, bigger, bag (voiced)

FRICATIVES (air flows through creating friction/noise):
- /f/ - fine, offer, off (voiceless)
- /v/ - vine, saving, of (voiced)
- /s/ - seal, missing, face (voiceless)
- /z/ - zeal, crazy, phase (voiced)
- /ʃ/ - show, pushing, rush (voiceless)
- /ʒ/ - measure, vision (voiced)
- /tʃ/ - choke, watching, catch
- /dʒ/ - joke, damage, large

NASALS (sound comes through the nose):
- /m/ - moon, summer, dome
- /n/ - nose, dinner, sun
- /ŋ/ - sing, ring, belong

KEY CONCEPTS:
- Place of articulation = WHERE the sound is made (lips, teeth, tongue position)
- Manner of articulation = HOW the sound is made (blocked, friction, etc.)
- Voicing = WHETHER vocal cords vibrate (voiced or voiceless)

SECTION 2: READING COMPREHENSION & TECHNIQUES
================================================================================

A. THREE MAIN READING TECHNIQUES:

1. SCANNING
   - Look for specific information quickly
   - Like searching for a phone number in a directory
   - Skip unnecessary words
   - Focus on keywords and numbers
   - Good for finding: dates, names, numbers, specific facts

2. SKIMMING
   - Read to get the main idea/general impression
   - Look at titles, headings, first and last paragraphs
   - Read the opening sentences of paragraphs
   - Understand overall content without details
   - Good for: getting an overview, deciding if text is relevant

3. CLOSE READING
   - Careful, detailed reading
   - Note specific details and meanings
   - Look for figurative language and symbolism
   - Understand word choice and author's intent
   - Steps:
     1. Read the text completely
     2. Annotate (highlight, make notes)
     3. Reflect on what you read
     4. Read again focusing on patterns and organization

B. TIPS FOR EFFECTIVE READING:
- Pay attention to titles, subheadings, illustrations
- Look for topic sentences and concluding sentences
- Use text features like bold words, italics, numbered lists
- Connect text to your personal life
- Ask questions while reading: Who? What? Where? When? Why? How?
- Take notes while reading
- Re-read difficult sections

C. COMPREHENSION STRATEGIES:
- Predict what will happen next
- Make connections to other texts or your experiences
- Summarize main ideas
- Clarify confusing parts
- Ask questions about the text
- Look for cause and effect relationships

SECTION 3: GRAMMAR USAGE
================================================================================

A. PARTS OF SPEECH (9 Main Types)

1. NOUNS - Words that name people, places, things, or ideas
   
   Types of Nouns:
   - Common nouns: chair, book, boy, girl, dog (lowercase)
   - Proper nouns: Ghana, Accra, Adwoa, Kofi, Monday (ALWAYS capitalized)
   - Concrete nouns: table, phone, window, car (physical objects you can touch)
   - Abstract nouns: love, kindness, honesty, peace, justice (ideas/feelings)
   - Collective nouns: family, team, group, class, army (groups of things)
   
   Forms of Nouns:
   - Singular: one cat, one boy
   - Plural: two cats, two boys
     * Regular: add -s (cat→cats) or -es (box→boxes)
     * Irregular: man→men, child→children, sheep→sheep
   
   Functions of Nouns in Sentences:
   - Subject (who/what the sentence is about): "The cat is sleeping"
   - Direct object (receives the action): "I see a dog"
   - Indirect object (to/for whom): "Give me the book"
   - Subject complement (describes the subject): "She is a teacher"
   
   Possessive Forms (showing ownership):
   - Singular noun: Akwasi's bag, Adwoa's dress, the boy's shoe
   - Plural noun: the boys' dormitory, the girls' house, the students' books
   - If noun ends in 's': James' bag or James's bag, Abass' book

2. PRONOUNS - Words that replace nouns to avoid repetition
   
   Types of Pronouns:
   - Personal: I, you, he, she, it, we, they (subjects)
              me, you, him, her, it, us, them (objects)
   - Possessive: mine, yours, his, hers, its, ours, theirs
   - Relative: who, whom, whose, which, that (connect clauses)
   - Interrogative: who, what, when, where, why, which (ask questions)
   - Reflexive: myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, 
              themselves (refer back to subject)
   - Demonstrative: this, that, these, those (point to specific things)

3. ADJECTIVES - Words that describe or modify nouns
   
   Types:
   - Descriptive: beautiful, tall, red, smart, happy, terrible
   - Quantitative: many, few, several, some, all, few, less, more
   
   Degrees of Adjectives:
   - Absolute: describes alone, not compared
     "a beautiful sunset"
   - Comparative: compares two things, usually add -er or use "more"
     "She is taller than her brother"
     "This book is more interesting than that one"
   - Superlative: compares three or more, add -est or use "most"
     "She is the tallest student in class"
     "This is the most interesting book I've read"
   
   Rules for forming comparatives and superlatives:
   - One-syllable adjectives: add -er/-est
     tall → taller → tallest
     big → bigger → biggest
   - Two-syllable adjectives ending in -y: replace y with -ier/-iest
     happy → happier → happiest
     pretty → prettier → prettiest
   - Two-syllable adjectives ending in -er, -le, -ow: add -er/-est
     clever → cleverer → cleverest
   - Three+ syllable adjectives: use "more"/"most"
     beautiful → more beautiful → most beautiful
   
   Uses of Adjectives:
   - Before noun (attributive): "The red car is fast"
   - After linking verbs (is, am, are, feels, seems, tastes, looks): 
     "The food tastes awful" / "She seems happy"

4. VERBS - Action words or words showing state of being
   
   Types:
   - Action verbs: run, jump, sing, write, dance, eat, sleep
   - Linking verbs: is, am, are, was, were, be, being, been, seem, appear, become
   - Auxiliary (helping) verbs: do, does, did, have, has, had, be, is, am, are, 
                               can, could, will, would, may, might, must, should
   
   Regular vs. Irregular Verbs:
   - Regular: add -ed for past tense
     walk → walked, talk → talked, play → played
   - Irregular: change completely
     go → went, eat → ate, see → saw, come → came, write → wrote, bring → brought
   
   VERB TENSES:
   
   PRESENT TENSE:
   
   Simple Present (habits, facts, routines):
   - I walk, you walk, he/she walks, we walk, they walk
   - "Every day I walk to school"
   - "She drinks tea in the morning"
   
   Present Continuous (happening right now):
   - I am walking, you are walking, he/she is walking, we are walking
   - Form: am/is/are + base+ing
   - "I am studying right now"
   - "They are playing football"
   
   Present Perfect (started in past, continues or just finished):
   - I have walked, you have walked, he/she has walked
   - Form: have/has + past participle (-ed)
   - "I have lived here for 5 years" (still living here)
   - "She has just arrived" (just finished arriving)
   
   Present Perfect Continuous (happening from past to now):
   - I have been walking, you have been walking, he/she has been walking
   - Form: have/has + been + base+ing
   - "I have been studying for 2 hours"
   - "She has been waiting all day"
   
   PAST TENSE:
   
   Simple Past (completed action in past):
   - Regular: walked, talked, played
   - Irregular: went, ate, saw, came, wrote
   - "Yesterday I walked to school"
   - "Last week she ate delicious food"
   
   Past Continuous (was happening when something else occurred):
   - I was walking, you were walking, he/she was walking
   - Form: was/were + base+ing
   - "I was studying when he called"
   - "They were playing when it started raining"
   
   Past Perfect (happened before another past action):
   - I had walked, you had walked, he/she had walked
   - Form: had + past participle
   - "She had eaten before he arrived"
   
   FUTURE TENSE:
   
   Simple Future (will happen):
   - I will walk, you will walk, he/she will walk
   - Form: will + base verb
   - "Tomorrow I will go to school"
   - "Next week they will start their exams"
   
   Future Continuous:
   - I will be walking, you will be walking
   - Form: will + be + base+ing
   - "This time next week, I will be studying"
   
   Future Perfect:
   - I will have walked, you will have walked
   - Form: will + have + past participle
   - "By next month, I will have finished this book"

5. ADVERBS - Words that modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs
   - Quickly, slowly, carefully, happily, unfortunately, very, too, really
   - They usually answer: How? When? Where? How much? To what extent?
   - "She runs quickly" (how)
   - "They will come tomorrow" (when)
   - "We went outside" (where)

6. PREPOSITIONS - Show relationships between words and other words
   
   Simple Prepositions:
   - Place: in, on, at, by, from, to, with, without, above, below, near, far, 
           inside, outside, beside, between, among, under, over, across
   - Time: in, at, on, during, after, before, since, for, until, by
   - Direction: to, from, into, out of, up, down, toward, away from
   
   Complex Prepositions (multi-word):
   - in front of, behind, because of, in spite of, due to, instead of, on behalf of,
     out of, in view of, by means of, as a result of, apart from, despite, close to
   
   Uses:
   - Place: "The book is on the table"
   - Time: "We will meet at 3 o'clock"
   - Reason: "Because of the rain, we stayed home"
   - Manner: "He did it with care"

7. CONJUNCTIONS - Words that connect other words, phrases, or clauses
   
   Coordinating Conjunctions (FANBOYS):
   - For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So
   - Connect equal ideas
   - "I wanted to go, but it was raining"
   - "She likes tea and coffee"
   
   Subordinating Conjunctions:
   - Because, since, as, although, though, while, if, unless, when, after, before,
     as if, even though, in case, provided that
   - Connect unequal ideas
   - "Although it was cold, we went outside"
   - "I will come if you invite me"
   
   Correlative Conjunctions:
   - Either...or, neither...nor, both...and, not only...but also, as...as
   - "Either you study or you fail"
   - "Both Anna and Maria passed the exam"

8. DETERMINERS - Words that determine/specify nouns and make them clear
   
   Types:
   - Articles: a, an (indefinite - unknown), the (definite - known)
     "I saw a cat" (one cat, don't know which)
     "The cat is sleeping" (specific cat we know about)
   
   - Possessives: my, your, his, her, our, its, their
     "Her book is on the table"
   
   - Demonstratives: this, that, these, those
     "This book is mine" / "That house is far away"
   
   - Quantifiers: some, many, few, several, all, each, every, both, either, 
                 neither, much, little, less, more
     "Some students passed the exam"
     "Every student must study hard"

9. INTERJECTIONS - Exclamations expressing sudden emotion or attention
   - Alas! (sadness, pity)
   - Ah! (realization, surprise)
   - Hey! (attention, surprise)
   - Wow! (amazement, admiration)
   - Ouch! (pain)
   - Phew! (relief, exhaustion)
   - Uh! (hesitation)
   - Well... (introduction)
   - Oh dear! (pity, surprise)
   - Hurray! (joy, celebration)

B. SENTENCE STRUCTURE:

Basic Sentence Structure: Subject + Verb + Object
- "The teacher (subject) teaches (verb) English (object)"
- "I (subject) love (verb) reading (object)"

Types of Sentences:
- Simple: one independent clause
  "The cat is sleeping"
- Compound: two independent clauses joined by coordinating conjunction
  "The cat is sleeping, but the dog is playing"
- Complex: independent clause + dependent clause with subordinating conjunction
  "Although the cat is sleeping, the dog is playing"

SECTION 4: WRITING SKILLS
================================================================================

A. PARAGRAPH STRUCTURE:

Every good paragraph has THREE main parts:

1. TOPIC SENTENCE (usually first)
   - States the main idea
   - What the paragraph is about
   
2. SUPPORTING SENTENCES (middle)
   - Explain and develop the topic sentence
   - Give details, examples, reasons
   - Usually 3-5 sentences
   
3. CONCLUDING SENTENCE (last)
   - Summarizes or restates the main idea
   - Can give a final comment or opinion
   - Leads to the next paragraph

EXAMPLE PARAGRAPH:
Topic Sentence: "Road accidents have caused a lot of havoc in Ghana."
Supporting: "These mostly result from illegal overtaking and drunk driving."
Supporting: "Excessive speeding has played a major role."
Supporting: "Drivers must follow traffic rules to prevent these accidents."
Concluding: "Only through proper enforcement can we reduce these tragedies."

B. COHESIVE DEVICES (Linking words that connect ideas):

Adding Information:
- also, furthermore, moreover, in addition, besides, additionally, as well as

Showing Results:
- therefore, thus, consequently, as a result, so, for this reason, this leads to

Showing Contrast:
- however, but, yet, in contrast, although, though, on the other hand, whereas

Showing Time/Order:
- first, second, then, next, finally, lastly, meanwhile, previously, initially

Giving Examples:
- for example, for instance, such as, like, including, namely

Restating/Clarifying:
- in other words, that is, in summary, to summarize, in brief, in conclusion

Showing Purpose:
- so that, in order to, to, for the purpose of, with the aim of

Showing Reason:
- because, since, as, due to, owing to, because of, on account of

C. ESSAY STRUCTURE:

INTRODUCTION (First Paragraph)
- Hook: interesting sentence to grab reader's attention
- Background information: context for your topic
- Thesis statement: your main argument/position
- Preview: mention main points you'll discuss

BODY PARAGRAPHS (Middle)
Each body paragraph should:
- Have a topic sentence (main point)
- Have 2-4 supporting sentences (evidence, examples, explanations)
- Have a concluding sentence (summary of paragraph)
- Use linking words to connect ideas

Paragraph 1: First main point
Paragraph 2: Second main point
Paragraph 3: Third main point (if needed)

CONCLUSION (Last Paragraph)
- Restate thesis (say it differently, don't just copy)
- Summarize main points
- Final thought: implication, call to action, or broader perspective
- Never introduce new information in conclusion

D. TYPES OF WRITING:

1. DESCRIPTIVE WRITING
   Purpose: Paint a picture with words
   Techniques:
   - Use vivid, specific adjectives
   - Use sensory details (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch)
   - Use figurative language:
     * Simile: comparison using "like" or "as"
       "The clouds were as white as snow"
     * Metaphor: direct comparison without "like" or "as"
       "Time is money"
     * Personification: giving human qualities to non-human things
       "The wind whispered through the trees"
     * Imagery: creating mental pictures
   
   Example: Instead of "The sunset was beautiful"
   Write: "The crimson and golden sunset painted the sky with brilliant hues,
            gradually fading to deep purple as darkness approached"

2. NARRATIVE WRITING
   Purpose: Tell a story
   Elements:
   - Characters: who is in the story
   - Setting: where and when the story takes place
   - Plot: what happens (beginning, middle, end)
   - Conflict: the problem or challenge
   - Resolution: how the problem is solved
   - Point of view: who tells the story
   
   Story Structure:
   - Exposition: introduce characters, setting, situation
   - Rising action: events leading toward the climax
   - Climax: turning point, most exciting moment
   - Falling action: events after climax
   - Resolution: how story ends
   
   Writing Tips:
   - Usually use past tense
   - Use dialogue to show character voices
   - Use linking words like "then," "next," "suddenly," "meanwhile"
   - Show emotions and reactions

3. PERSUASIVE WRITING
   Purpose: Convince the reader of your position
   Techniques:
   - Strong opening with clear thesis
   - Logical arguments with evidence
   - Appeal to emotion when appropriate
   - Acknowledge opposing views
   - Use persuasive language ("must," "should," "clearly," "obviously")
   - Strong conclusion that reinforces your position
   
   Structure:
   - Introduction: state your position clearly
   - Body: give 2-3 strong reasons with evidence
   - Counterargument: mention opposing view
   - Refutation: explain why opposition is wrong
   - Conclusion: restate position strongly

4. EXPOSITORY WRITING
   Purpose: Explain or inform
   Characteristics:
   - Clear and straightforward
   - Presents facts, not opinions
   - Uses examples and definitions
   - Organized logically
   - Objective tone
   - Answers "why" and "how" questions

E. LETTER WRITING:

INFORMAL LETTER (to friends/family):

Format:
Your Address (top left)
Date
Dear [Friend's name],

[Opening - Say why you're writing]

[Body - Main message with details, news, feelings]

[Closing - Final friendly thought]

Your loving friend,
[Your name]

Features:
- Casual, friendly tone
- Use contractions (I'm, you're, don't)
- Conversational language
- Personal details and feelings
- Can be about news, gratitude, invitation, or just staying in touch

Example:
23 Oxford Street
Accra
15 March 2026

Dear Kwame,

How are you? I hope you're doing well. I haven't heard from you in ages! I wanted to 
write and tell you about my new school. It's fantastic, and I've made some great friends 
already.

I've been very busy with homework, but I love most of my classes. English is my favorite - 
the teacher makes it so interesting! I miss seeing you though. We should meet up soon 
and catch up properly.

How's your new job going? I'd love to hear all about it. Let me know when you're free 
and we can arrange something.

Looking forward to hearing from you soon!

Your old friend,
Ama

F. PRECISE WORDS AND PHRASES:
Use exact, specific words instead of vague ones.

Instead of: "got there" → Use: "arrived"
Instead of: "put in" → Use: "installed" or "deposited"  
Instead of: "got to see" → Use: "realized" or "found out"
Instead of: "made up" → Use: "fabricated" or "invented"
Instead of: "old" → Use: "ancient," "vintage," "worn," or "mature"

This makes your writing more powerful and clear.

SECTION 5: LITERATURE IN ENGLISH
================================================================================

A. WHAT IS LITERATURE?
Literature is any written or oral work with imaginative or artistic value.
It's created to express ideas, emotions, experiences, and cultural values through 
language in beautiful or meaningful ways.

Important because it:
- Entertains us
- Preserves culture and traditions
- Teaches moral lessons
- Provides education
- Can be a source of income for authors
- Helps us understand ourselves and others

B. TYPES OF LITERATURE:

1. ORAL LITERATURE
   How it's passed: By word of mouth, through speaking and listening
   Examples:
   - Folktales: traditional stories about people and places
   - Folklore: customs, beliefs, traditions of a group
   - Proverbs: short wise sayings
   - Myths: stories about gods and ancient times
   - Legends: stories about historical or semi-historical figures
   - Dirges/Eulogies: speeches praising the dead
   - Riddles: puzzles to solve
   - Songs: musical compositions with words
   - Dances: movement telling stories or expressing emotion
   - Festivals: cultural celebrations with stories and traditions
   
   Why it matters: Keeps culture alive, connects generations

2. WRITTEN LITERATURE
   How it's passed: Through written text, fixed in books
   Examples:
   - Novels: long fictional narratives
   - Short stories: brief fictional narratives
   - Poems: artistic expression in lines and verses
   - Plays/Drama: stories told through dialogue and action
   - Essays: short pieces of non-fiction writing
   - Articles: informative pieces on specific topics

C. GENRES (Main types) OF LITERATURE:

1. PROSE - Regular narrative writing without specific structure
   
   Fiction (made-up stories):
   - Novels: "The Beautiful Ones Are Not Yet Born" by Ayi Kwei Armah
   - Short stories: "The Kaya Girl" by Mamle Wolo
   - Characteristics: characters, plot, setting, dialogue
   
   Non-Fiction (true accounts):
   - Autobiographies
   - Biographies
   - Essays
   - Memoirs

2. POETRY - Writing that uses special language and structure
   - Written in lines and verses (not regular sentences)
   - Uses rhythm, rhyme, and sound patterns
   - Expresses emotions or ideas concisely
   - Uses figurative language heavily
   - Examples: "Makola" by Theresah Ennin, "Skirt" by Niyi Osundare

3. DRAMA - Story told through dialogue and movement
   - Has characters speaking to each other (dialogue)
   - Has stage directions showing actions
   - Divided into scenes and acts
   - Usually meant to be performed on stage
   - Types:
     * Tragedy: sad ending, character's downfall
     * Comedy: funny, happy ending, light situations
     * Farce: extreme humor, ridiculous situations
   - Examples:
     * "The Blinkards" by Kobina Sekyi
     * "The Son Umbele" by Bill Marshall
     * "The Marriage of Anansewa" by Efua T. Sutherland
     * "A Tribe Called Judah" by Funke Akindele

D. SHORT STORY ELEMENTS:

1. PLOT - The sequence of events that make up the story
   
   Parts of Plot:
   - Exposition: Introduction of characters, setting, and situation
     What we need to know to start
   
   - Rising Action: Events that develop the conflict and build tension
     Things get more complicated and interesting
   
   - Climax: The turning point, most exciting moment
     The moment of greatest tension
     The reader wonders "what will happen now?"
   
   - Falling Action: Events after climax where tension decreases
     Problems start to be resolved
   
   - Resolution/Denouement: How the story ends
     The final outcome for characters

2. CHARACTER - The people, animals, or beings in the story
   
   Main Types:
   - Protagonist: The main character (hero/heroine)
     Central to the plot, reader usually roots for them
   
   - Antagonist: Character opposing the protagonist
     The "villain" or opposing force
     Doesn't have to be evil, just in opposition
   
   Characteristics:
   - Round Character: Complex, develops throughout story
     Shows different emotions and sides of personality
     We learn a lot about them
   
   - Flat Character: Simple, stays basically the same
     Doesn't develop much
     Has one or two main traits
   
   - Dynamic Character: Changes significantly throughout story
     Is transformed by events
   
   - Static Character: Doesn't change
     Same at end as at beginning

3. SETTING - The time and place where the story happens
   
   Includes:
   - Physical location: Ghana, rural village, Accra, school
   - Time period: 1960s, modern day, future, at night
   - Social environment: wealthy, poor, war, peace
   - Mood/atmosphere: happy, sad, scary, mysterious
   
   Why it matters:
   - Affects what characters can do
   - Creates mood
   - May create conflict itself

4. CONFLICT - The main problem or struggle in the story
   
   Types:
   - Character vs. Character (external)
     Two or more characters fighting each other
     "John fights with his brother over the inheritance"
   
   - Character vs. Self (internal)
     Person struggling with their own feelings, choices, desires
     "Maria struggles with whether to pursue her dreams or please her parents"
   
   - Character vs. Nature (external)
     Person fighting against natural forces
     "The survivors battle the desert to reach safety"
   
   - Character vs. Society (external)
     Person fighting against rules, customs, or expectations of society
     "The student rebels against strict school rules"

5. THEME - The main message or central idea of the story
   
   What the author is saying about life or human nature
   
   Examples:
   - "Good overcomes evil"
   - "Honesty is the best policy"
   - "Love conquers all"
   - "Greed leads to destruction"
   - "Education is powerful"
   - "Never judge by appearance"
   - "Family is important"
   
   How to find theme:
   - What does the character learn?
   - What problem is solved?
   - What message does the ending give?
   - What does the title suggest?

6. POINT OF VIEW - Who tells the story
   
   First Person (I, we):
   - The main character tells the story
   - "I walked into the room and saw..."
   - We only know what this character knows/feels
   - Creates intimacy with reader
   - Limited perspective (can't know others' thoughts)
   
   Second Person (You):
   - Rare in fiction
   - Reader is the character
   - "You walk into the room..."
   - Creates direct engagement
   
   Third Person Omniscient (He/She/They):
   - Narrator knows everything about all characters
   - Can tell thoughts and feelings of multiple characters
   - "John left while Mary waited, thinking..."
   - Most flexible point of view
   
   Third Person Limited:
   - Narrator tells about one character's perspective
   - Can only know that character's thoughts
   - "He left while she waited, wondering what he thought..."
   - Creates some mystery

E. CHARACTERIZATION - How an author shows what a character is like:

Direct Characterization:
- Author directly tells us about the character
- "John was very brave and honest"
- "She was mean and jealous"

Indirect Characterization (showing through):
- What the character says (dialogue)
  "I will never give up!" shows determination
  
- What the character does (actions)
  Helping others shows kindness
  
- How the character looks (appearance)
  Neat appearance shows care
  
- What others say about them
  "She's the smartest student" - shows intelligence
  
- Internal thoughts/feelings
  "He worried constantly" - shows anxiety

F. MONOLOGUE VS. DIALOGUE:

MONOLOGUE - One person speaking (alone or to audience/another)

Types:
- Soliloquy: Character speaks to themselves, reveals innermost thoughts
  Like thinking out loud
  Common in Shakespeare
  "To be or not to be..." (Hamlet)
  
- Dramatic Monologue: Character speaks to audience or another character
  Long speech, unbroken
  Reveals character's personality
  
- Interior/Internal Monologue: Character's thoughts shown
  Written on stage/page for audience to see
  Shows what character is thinking while something else happens

DIALOGUE - Conversation between two or more people

Types:
- Outer Dialogue: Between characters
  Regular conversation in story
  Shows how characters interact
  
- Inner Dialogue: Character talking to themselves
  "Should I do this?" they wonder
  Shows internal struggle

Why Dialogue is Important:
- Reveals character personality
- Advances the plot
- Shows relationships between characters
- Makes story feel real and immediate
- Breaks up long descriptions

SECTION 6: LANGUAGE USE IN DIFFERENT CONTEXTS
================================================================================

A. FORMAL vs. INFORMAL LANGUAGE:

FORMAL LANGUAGE
When to use: Professional settings, official documents, job interviews, court, 
            formal presentations, writing to authority figures

Vocabulary Differences:
Informal          →  Formal
Tell me more      →  Elaborate
Show             →  Demonstrate
Give me          →  Provide
Make up          →  Fabricate / Fabricate
Plus             →  Furthermore / Moreover
So               →  Thus / Therefore
Also             →  Additionally
But              →  However
Huge             →  Enormous / Substantial
Tiny             →  Diminutive / Minute
Rude             →  Disagreeable / Impolite
Nice             →  Pleasant / Agreeable
Friendly         →  Amiable / Cordial
Messed up        →  Damaged / Compromised
Don't have       →  Lack / Do not possess
Okay             →  Acceptable / Satisfactory

Other Formal Features:
- Complete sentences (no contractions: use "is not" not "isn't")
- Complex sentence structures
- Objective tone
- Academic vocabulary
- Proper punctuation throughout
- No slang or colloquialisms

Example of Formal Language:
"I am writing to inquire about the position you have recently advertised. 
I believe my qualifications and experience make me an excellent candidate for 
this role. I have enclosed my resume and cover letter for your consideration."

INFORMAL LANGUAGE
When to use: Friends, family, casual conversations, text messages, personal emails

Characteristics:
- Casual vocabulary
- Shorter sentences
- Contractions (it's, don't, won't)
- Slang acceptable
- Relaxed tone
- Can be incomplete sentences
- Can use emojis in text/emails

Example of Informal Language:
"Hey! I'm excited about the party next weekend. I can't wait to see you! 
Bring your friends if you want. It's gonna be fun!"

REGISTER EXAMPLE - Different ways to say you need the bathroom:
1. Very Formal (to a government official):
   "May I be excused to utilize the facilities?"

2. Formal (to your teacher):
   "May I be excused to the restroom, please?"

3. Semi-formal (to your boss/colleague):
   "I need to use the restroom for a moment"

4. Informal (to close friends):
   "I gotta go to the bathroom"

5. Very Informal (to family):
   "I'm going to the loo"

KEY POINT: The same word or idea has different expressions depending on 
the formality level needed for the situation.

A. HOW TO MEMORIZE VOWEL SOUNDS:

Create sentences with each sound and say them aloud:

/i:/ (Long E sound) = "See the green tree by the sea in the breeze"
/ɪ/ (Short I sound) = "The big pig sits in the pit with his friends"
/e/ (Short E sound) = "The red net was set in the nest on the bed"
/æ/ (Short A sound) = "The man sat with a can and a fat black cat"
/a:/ (Long A sound) = "The art class starts in the park after dark"
/ɒ/ (Short O sound) = "The pot is on top with a hot dog inside"
/ɔ:/ (Long O sound) = "The tall girl calls from the court for the door"
/ʊ/ (Short U sound) = "The good cook put the book in the foot"
/u:/ (Long OO sound) = "The pool is cool and the food is delicious"
/ʌ/ (Short U sound) = "The bus is full of mud and the cut is fun"
/ɜ:/ (Ur sound) = "The bird heard the nurse work hard for nurse"
/ə/ (Neutral sound) = "The teacher is around the sofa in about an hour"

PRACTICE TIP: Say each sentence out loud 5-10 times. The mouth movements 
and sounds together help memory stick better.

B. HOW TO IDENTIFY PARTS OF SPEECH:

NOUN TEST: Can you put "the" before it?
✓ "The book" = noun
✓ "The tree" = noun
✓ "The happiness" = noun
✗ "The quickly" = not a noun
✗ "The run" = hmm, actually "the run" CAN be a noun...

VERB TEST: Is it an action or state?
✓ "run, jump, sleep, think, eat" = verbs
✓ "is, am, are" = verbs (state of being)
✗ "book, happy, fast" = not verbs

ADJECTIVE TEST: Does it describe a noun?
✓ "beautiful sunset" = beautiful describes sunset
✓ "quickly ran" = wait, NO - quickly describes HOW (adverb)
✓ "angry student" = angry describes student

ADVERB TEST: Does it describe how/when/where?
✓ "runs quickly" = quickly says HOW he runs
✓ "came yesterday" = yesterday says WHEN
✓ "played outside" = outside says WHERE

C. REMEMBERING COORDINATING CONJUNCTIONS:

Use the acronym: FANBOYS
F - For
A - And
N - Nor
B - But
O - Or
Y - Yet
S - So

Remember: "I like both fanboys and coordinating conjunctions!"

D. TENSE QUICK REFERENCE GUIDE:

PRESENT TENSE:
- SIMPLE PRESENT: "I walk" (Every day/habit/fact)
- PRESENT CONTINUOUS: "I am walking" (Right now/at this moment)
- PRESENT PERFECT: "I have walked" (Just finished/started in past)
- PRESENT PERFECT CONTINUOUS: "I have been walking" (For how long)

PAST TENSE:
- SIMPLE PAST: "I walked" (Yesterday/last week/finished)
- PAST CONTINUOUS: "I was walking" (When something happened)
- PAST PERFECT: "I had walked" (Before another past event)
- PAST PERFECT CONTINUOUS: "I had been walking" (For how long before)

FUTURE TENSE:
- SIMPLE FUTURE: "I will walk" (Tomorrow/next week/will do)
- FUTURE CONTINUOUS: "I will be walking" (While something happens)
- FUTURE PERFECT: "I will have walked" (By a certain time)

TIME WORDS TO REMEMBER WITH EACH:
- Present Simple: every, always, often, sometimes, usually, now
- Present Continuous: now, right now, at this moment, at present
- Past Simple: yesterday, last week, ago, the other day, in 1990
- Future Simple: tomorrow, next week, next year, will, going to

E. READING TECHNIQUES QUICK GUIDE:

SCANNING - Finding specific information:
✓ Need to find a name, date, number? SCAN
✓ Looking for one specific fact? SCAN
✓ Skip unimportant words
✓ Focus on keywords

SKIMMING - Getting the main idea:
✓ Need an overview? SKIM
✓ Checking if text is relevant? SKIM

CLOSE READING - Deep understanding:
✓ Need to analyze/understand deeply? CLOSE READ

✓ Look for patterns
✓ Consider author's choices

F. PARAGRAPH STRUCTURE TRICK:

T.S. = Topic Sentence (What paragraph is about)

Quick checklist for every paragraph:

H. COMMON EXAM MISTAKES TO AVOID:

1. SPELLING ERRORS:
   ✗ "there/their/they're" confused
   ✗ "its/it's" mixed up
   ✗ "you're/your" swapped
   TIP: Double-check these commonly confused words

2. GRAMMAR ERRORS:
   ✗ "I goed" instead of "I went"
   ✗ "He don't know" instead of "He doesn't know"
   ✗ Subject-verb disagreement
   TIP: Know irregular verb forms

3. VOCABULARY ERRORS:
   ✗ Using vague words: "good," "bad," "nice," "thing"
   ✗ Using informal language in formal essay
   TIP: Use specific, precise vocabulary

4. PUNCTUATION ERRORS:
   ✗ Missing commas in compound sentences
   ✗ No period at end of sentence
   ✗ Apostrophes in wrong place
   TIP: Read aloud to find punctuation needs

5. CAPITALIZATION ERRORS:
   ✗ Not capitalizing proper nouns (Ghana, Monday, Accra)
   ✗ Random capitalization in middle of sentence
   TIP: Remember: names of places, people, days, months always capital

6. TENSE INCONSISTENCY:
   ✗ Mixing past and present tense in story
   TIP: Choose tense and stick with it (most narratives use past)

7. RUN-ON SENTENCES:
   ✗ "I went to school and I studied hard and I took a test and I passed"
   TIP: Use periods or linking words to separate ideas

8. SENTENCE FRAGMENTS:
   ✗ "Because it was raining." (not a complete sentence)
   TIP: Make sure every sentence has subject and verb

9. LINKING WORDS:
   ✗ Not using linking words between ideas
   ✗ Ideas seem disconnected
   TIP: Use "however," "therefore," "first," "finally," etc.

10. NOT FOLLOWING INSTRUCTIONS:
    ✗ Writing more or less than required
    ✗ Answering different question than asked
    TIP: Read questions carefully multiple times

   - Check you answered the question
   - Add anything you missed
